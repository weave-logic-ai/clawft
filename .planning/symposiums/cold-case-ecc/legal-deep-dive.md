## Topic 1: Florida's Daubert Standard

### 1.1 Adoption History: From Frye to Daubert (and Back, and Back Again)

Florida's path to the Daubert standard was not straightforward. It involved legislative action, Supreme Court resistance, judicial retirements, and ultimately a reversal of a reversal. The full timeline:

**Pre-2013: The Frye Era**

Florida courts operated under the *Frye* test, derived from *Frye v. United States*, 293 F. 1013 (D.C. Cir. 1923). Under Frye, expert testimony based on a scientific technique was admissible only if the technique was "sufficiently established to have gained general acceptance in the particular field in which it belongs." This was the sole gatekeeping standard for scientific evidence in Florida for decades.

**July 1, 2013: Legislative Adoption of Daubert**

The Florida Legislature passed **HB 7015** (Ch. 2013-107), which amended **Fla. Stat. section 90.702** to adopt the Daubert standard. The bill passed the Florida Senate on April 26, 2013 (30-9 vote) and the House accepted the Senate version the same day (70-41 vote). Governor Rick Scott signed it into law, effective **July 1, 2013**.

The preamble to HB 7015 expressly stated that the Legislature intended to "adopt the standards for expert testimony in the courts of this state as provided in":
- *Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579 (1993)
- *General Electric Co. v. Joiner*, 522 U.S. 136 (1997)
- *Kumho Tire Co. v. Carmichael*, 526 U.S. 137 (1999)

The amended section 90.702 adopted, almost verbatim, Federal Rule of Evidence 702.

**February 16, 2017: Supreme Court Declines to Adopt**

The Florida Supreme Court declined to adopt the Daubert amendment as a procedural rule of court, holding that the question of whether the amendment was procedural (and therefore within the judiciary's exclusive rulemaking authority) required further analysis. *In re: Amendments to the Florida Evidence Code*, No. SC16-181 (Fla. Feb. 16, 2017).

**October 15, 2018: Supreme Court Strikes Down Daubert**

In *DeLisle v. Crane Co.*, No. SC16-2182 (Fla. Oct. 15, 2018), the Florida Supreme Court issued a 4-3 decision holding that the Legislature's adoption of Daubert was **unconstitutional** because the standard for admissibility of expert testimony is a procedural rule within the sole purview of the judiciary, and the Legislature could not change it through ordinary legislation. The Court held that the Frye standard remained the appropriate test in Florida.

**May 23, 2019: Supreme Court Reverses Itself, Adopts Daubert**

Shortly after *DeLisle*, three members of the majority — Justices Pariente, Lewis, and Quince — reached Florida's mandatory judicial retirement age and retired. Governor Ron DeSantis appointed their replacements. The reconstituted Court, in *In re: Amendments to the Florida Evidence Code*, No. SC19-107 (Fla. May 23, 2019), reversed its prior decisions and **adopted the Daubert standard under the Court's own rulemaking authority**. The Daubert standard has been operative in Florida since May 23, 2019.

**Current Status (2026)**: Florida is a Daubert state. The standard applies to all expert testimony in Florida courts, both civil and criminal.

### 1.2 What Daubert Requires

#### The Four Daubert Factors

*Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579 (1993), established that trial judges serve as "gatekeepers" for scientific evidence. The Court identified four non-exclusive factors for evaluating the admissibility of expert scientific testimony:

1. **Testability**: Whether the theory or technique can be (and has been) tested. "Scientific methodology today is based on generating hypotheses and testing them to see if they can be falsified." 509 U.S. at 593.

2. **Peer Review and Publication**: Whether the theory or technique has been subjected to peer review and publication. While publication is not a "sine qua non" of admissibility, "submission to the scrutiny of the scientific community is a component of 'good science,' in part because it increases the likelihood that substantive flaws in methodology will be detected." 509 U.S. at 593-94.

3. **Known or Potential Error Rate**: The known or potential rate of error of the technique, and the existence and maintenance of standards controlling the technique's operation. 509 U.S. at 594.

4. **General Acceptance**: Whether the technique has achieved general acceptance in the relevant scientific community. This former Frye requirement was retained as one factor among several, not as the sole determinant. 509 U.S. at 594.

The Court emphasized that the inquiry is a **flexible one**, and that "the focus, of course, must be solely on principles and methodology, not on the conclusions that they generate." 509 U.S. at 595.

#### The Joiner Clarification

*General Electric Co. v. Joiner*, 522 U.S. 136 (1997), clarified the standard of appellate review: a trial court's decision to admit or exclude expert testimony under Daubert is reviewed for **abuse of discretion**. This gives trial judges considerable latitude in gatekeeping decisions.

#### The Kumho Tire Extension

*Kumho Tire Co. v. Carmichael*, 526 U.S. 137 (1999), extended Daubert's gatekeeping obligation beyond "scientific" knowledge to encompass **all expert testimony**, including testimony based on "technical" and "other specialized" knowledge. The Court held:

> "We conclude that Daubert's general holding — setting forth the trial judge's general 'gatekeeping' obligation — applies not only to testimony based on 'scientific' knowledge, but also to testimony based on 'technical' and 'other specialized' knowledge." 526 U.S. at 141.

The Court further held that the specific Daubert factors "may or may not be pertinent in assessing reliability, depending on the nature of the issue, the expert's particular expertise, and the subject of his testimony." 526 U.S. at 150. The trial court has broad latitude in deciding how to test an expert's reliability.

**Significance for ECC**: Under *Kumho Tire*, computational analysis — whether characterized as "scientific," "technical," or "other specialized" knowledge — is subject to Daubert gatekeeping. The ECC system's causal graph analysis, spectral analysis, and coherence scoring fall squarely within the domain of "technical or specialized knowledge" that requires Daubert vetting.

### 1.3 AI and Computational Analysis Under Florida Daubert

#### Current Landscape: No Florida Appellate Ruling Directly on AI-Assisted Evidence

As of April 2026, no published Florida appellate opinion has directly addressed the admissibility of AI-assisted investigative analysis under Daubert. However, the landscape is developing rapidly:

**Florida Court Orders on AI Disclosure (January 2026)**

Florida's 11th Judicial Circuit (Miami-Dade) and 17th Judicial Circuit (Broward) issued administrative orders in January 2026 requiring lawyers and self-represented litigants to **disclose and certify AI-generated court submissions**. The orders require that "when AI has been used or assisted in the use of preparation, researching, drafting pleadings, drafting documents, filing documents, and/or discovery requests, the document shall identify the specific tool used."

While these orders address attorney filings rather than evidentiary admissibility, they signal judicial awareness and increasing scrutiny of AI in legal proceedings.

**Florida Supreme Court Review of Evidence Code Amendments (2025)**

The Office of the State Courts Administrator warned in 2023 that deepfakes and synthetic media could strain authentication standards. Florida's 2025 procedural reforms and the Supreme Court's ongoing review of Evidence Code amendments (SC2025-0659) create an opening for direct engagement with AI evidence issues.

#### Analogous Technology Evidence in Florida Courts

**Cell-Site Location Information (CSLI)**

Following *Carpenter v. United States*, 585 U.S. ___ (2018) (holding that acquisition of historical CSLI requires a warrant under the Fourth Amendment), Florida courts have addressed CSLI evidence under Daubert. Notably, at least one Florida appellate court allowed a detective to testify about cell site coverage maps placing a defendant near a crime scene, even though the detective did not know the underlying algorithm or the error rate of the mapping software, finding that his "experience and training" made the testimony admissible. This case illustrates both the opportunity and the risk: courts may be willing to admit technologically-derived evidence but may not rigorously apply Daubert's error-rate and testability factors.

**Genetic Genealogy**

Florida law enforcement has used investigative genetic genealogy (IGG) successfully in multiple cold case prosecutions:
- The 1987 murder of Opal Weil in Pinellas County, solved in February 2023 through Parabon NanoLabs genealogical testing linking crime-scene DNA to suspect Michael Lapniewski Jr.
- The 1991 killing of Julia Wilbanks, solved in 2025 using genetic genealogy and a discarded straw.
- The 1975 double murder of Barbara Schreiber and Darlene Zetterower, solved almost 50 years later through DNA genealogy in 2025.
- Multiple cases involving Othram Labs forensic genetic genealogy, including a 2025 Tampa confession.

The Florida Department of Law Enforcement (FDLE) operates a dedicated Forensic/Investigative Genetic Genealogy unit. While IGG itself is well-established, the underlying computational matching algorithms remain subject to Daubert challenge.

**ShotSpotter / Acoustic Gunshot Detection**

ShotSpotter (now SoundThinking) evidence has faced Daubert and Frye challenges nationwide. The technology has survived fourteen Frye challenges and two Daubert challenges as of 2025. However:
- The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court in 2025 held that a Daubert-Lanigan hearing should be allowed if requested to examine the reliability of ShotSpotter's retrospective forensic use.
- A California appellate court reversed a conviction based on ShotSpotter evidence because the trial court failed to hold a pretrial reliability hearing.
- Concerns center on the proprietary nature of the algorithms, lack of independent scientific validation, and instances where ShotSpotter analysts modified results after police department requests.

**Relevance to ECC**: The ShotSpotter precedent is directly instructive. ECC's open-source, auditable methodology positions it favorably compared to ShotSpotter's proprietary black box. Where ShotSpotter has faced challenges around transparency and independent validation, ECC's design inherently addresses these concerns.

#### Precedent from Other Jurisdictions on Computational Forensic Tools

**Probabilistic Genotyping Software (TrueAllele, STRmix)**

The most extensive body of case law on algorithmic evidence in criminal cases involves probabilistic genotyping software. These cases are directly relevant to ECC:

- Courts have consistently **denied** defense requests for TrueAllele source code, with Pennsylvania Judge Jack Panella writing that "TrueAllele is proprietary software; it would not be possible to market TrueAllele if it were available for free."
- However, the tide has turned in some jurisdictions. An appellate court reversed denial of source code access, holding: "If the State chooses to utilize an expert who relies on novel probabilistic genotyping software to render DNA testimony, then defendant is entitled to access, under an appropriate protective order, to the software's source code and supporting software development and related documentation."
- Even peer reviewers of TrueAllele studies have never had access to the source code.
- A Harvard Journal of Law & Technology article characterized these tools as "black box algorithms" that raise serious confrontation clause concerns.

**Palantir Gotham**

Palantir's Gotham platform has been used by law enforcement agencies, including by NYPD and ICE. Legal challenges have raised Fifth and Fourteenth Amendment due process concerns due to lack of transparency. Courts have demonstrated skepticism, often preferring to rely on non-algorithmic evidence. In February 2023, the German Federal Constitutional Court ruled Palantir's use in Hesse and Hamburg was unconstitutional for violating informational self-determination rights.

A persistent problem: prosecutors and law enforcement agencies have been careful not to cite Palantir in evidentiary documents, making it difficult for civil liberties lawyers to bring constitutional challenges.

### 1.4 Florida-Specific Statutes

#### Fla. Stat. section 90.702 — Expert Testimony

As amended and adopted by the Florida Supreme Court (effective May 23, 2019), this section mirrors Federal Rule of Evidence 702. It provides that a witness qualified as an expert may testify in the form of an opinion if:

(a) The expert's scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will help the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue;
(b) The testimony is based on sufficient facts or data;
(c) The testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods; and
(d) The expert has reliably applied the principles and methods to the facts of the case.

#### Fla. Stat. section 90.704 — Basis of Opinion Testimony by Experts

This section governs the factual basis for expert opinions:
- Facts or data upon which an expert bases an opinion may be those perceived by, or made known to, the expert at or before trial.
- If the facts or data are of a type **reasonably relied upon by experts in the subject** to support the opinion, they need not be independently admissible.
- Otherwise inadmissible facts may not be disclosed to the jury unless the court determines their probative value substantially outweighs their prejudicial effect.
- A party opposing the expert may conduct **voir dire** on the underlying facts/data. If prima facie evidence shows the expert lacks a sufficient basis, the opinions are inadmissible unless the proponent establishes the underlying facts or data.

**Significance for ECC**: Section 90.704 allows an expert using ECC to base opinions on the system's computational outputs (causal graph scores, gap analysis, coherence metrics) if such data is "of a type reasonably relied upon by experts in the subject." This requires establishing that computational causal analysis is a recognized methodology in investigative analytics.

#### Fla. Stat. section 90.901-90.903 — Authentication

Digital evidence must be authenticated under section 90.901 (requiring evidence sufficient to support a finding that the matter in question is what its proponent claims). Florida currently lacks self-authentication rules for electronic records comparable to Federal Rules 902(13)-(14), forcing reliance on live testimony for routine digital files.

#### Florida Rule of Criminal Procedure 3.220 — Discovery

Rule 3.220 governs criminal discovery in Florida. Key provisions relevant to computational evidence:
- **3.220(b)(1)(J)**: Requires disclosure of "reports or statements of experts made in connection with the particular case, including results of physical or mental examinations and of scientific tests, experiments, or comparisons."
- **3.220(b)(1)(H)**: Addresses electronic surveillance and related documentation.
- **3.220(d)**: Allows the court to require "such other discovery to the parties as justice may require" on a showing of materiality.

**Significance for ECC**: Under 3.220(b)(1)(J), the prosecution would be required to disclose all ECC reports, scoring outputs, and analytical methodology. This obligation aligns with ECC's design philosophy of full transparency. The ExoChain audit trail provides a ready-made disclosure package.

## Topic 2: Proposed Federal Rule of Evidence 707

### 2.1 The Proposal: Machine-Generated Evidence

#### Official Status: This Is a Real, Official Proposal

Proposed Federal Rule of Evidence 707 is **not** merely an academic proposal. It is an official rule amendment proceeding through the formal Rules Enabling Act process under the authority of the Judicial Conference of the United States.

#### Text of Proposed Rule 707

> **Rule 707. Machine-Generated Evidence**
>
> When machine-generated evidence is offered without an expert witness and would be subject to Rule 702 if testified to by a witness, the court may admit the evidence only if it satisfies the requirements of Rule 702(a)-(d). This rule does not apply to the output of simple scientific instruments.

#### Committee Note Purpose

The Advisory Committee on Evidence Rules developed Rule 707 to prevent "the proponent of machine-generated evidence from evading the reliability requirements of Rule 702 by offering machine output directly, where the output would be subject to Rule 702 if rendered as an opinion by a human expert."

#### Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 2, 2025 | Advisory Committee on Evidence Rules met to discuss draft |
| June 10, 2025 | Committee on Rules of Practice and Procedure approved Rule 707 for public comment (vote: 8-1) |
| August 15, 2025 | Public comment period opened |
| February 16, 2026 | Public comment period closed |
| May 7, 2026 | Evidence Rules Committee scheduled vote |
| 2026 (est.) | If approved, proceeds to full Judicial Conference |
| 2027 (est.) | If Judicial Conference approves, Supreme Court transmits to Congress by May |
| December 1, 2027 (est.) | Earliest plausible effective date under Rules Enabling Act |

#### What Rule 707 Requires

Under the proposed rule, machine-generated evidence must satisfy **all four prongs of Rule 702**:

(a) The evidence will help the trier of fact understand the evidence or determine a fact in issue;
(b) The evidence is based on sufficient facts or data;
(c) The evidence is the product of reliable principles and methods; and
(d) The evidence reflects a reliable application of the principles and methods to the facts of the case.

The Committee Note identifies concerns about:
- **Algorithmic bias** in machine-generated inferences
- **Incomplete factual support** underlying AI outputs
- **Transparency gaps** that prevent meaningful adversarial testing

#### What Rule 707 Does NOT Cover

The rule explicitly excludes "the output of simple scientific instruments" — thermometers, breathalyzers used for simple readings, scales, etc. The distinction is between instruments that measure and machines that infer.

#### Significance for ECC

ECC's causal graph analysis, gap detection, and coherence scoring constitute "machine-generated evidence" that draws inferences from data. Under proposed Rule 707, ECC outputs offered without accompanying expert testimony would need to satisfy Rule 702(a)-(d) independently. This means the methodology must be demonstrably reliable, testable, and based on sufficient data — requirements that ECC's architecture is designed to meet.

However, the more practical pathway is to pair ECC outputs with expert witness testimony from a qualified analyst, in which case standard Rule 702 (not 707) governs admissibility.

### 2.2 Current Federal Landscape for AI Evidence

#### FRE 702: The 2023 Amendment

On **December 1, 2023**, an amendment to Federal Rule of Evidence 702 took effect, clarifying two points that courts had frequently misapplied:

1. **Burden of proof**: The proponent of expert testimony bears the burden of establishing admissibility by a **preponderance of the evidence** ("more likely than not").
2. **Gatekeeping obligation**: Courts must actively exercise their gatekeeping function rather than presuming expert testimony is admissible.

The amendment added the word "the proponent has demonstrated that" before the four admissibility requirements, making explicit that the proponent — not the opponent — carries the burden at every stage.

By 2025, federal circuit courts had begun embracing the tightened standard. The Harvard Law Review published a detailed analysis of the amended rule in Volume 138.

#### DOJ Policy on AI in Criminal Cases

The **DOJ Report on Artificial Intelligence and Criminal Justice** (December 3, 2024), submitted in response to Executive Order 14110, provides the most comprehensive federal policy framework for AI in criminal proceedings:

**Key Recommendations**:
- AI should **support, not replace**, human decision-making in criminal justice
- Agencies must establish **clear AI policies** and maintain **human expert oversight**
- Organizations should procure only **well-validated tools** with demonstrated accuracy
- **Vendor documentation** must be detailed; **restrictive licensing agreements** should be avoided
- **Bias evaluation** must be thorough
- **Pre-deployment**: independent audits, operational testing, clear guidelines
- **Post-deployment**: regular audits for accuracy and bias, reassessment of expanded applications
- **Community transparency**: disclose AI use and safeguards to communities

On forensic analysis specifically, the DOJ recognized that AI integration "represents a potential shift from subjective analysis toward more objective, reproducible approaches" but warned that this "introduces new challenges requiring careful validation and oversight."

**Policy Shift (January 2025)**: Executive Order 14148 (signed January 20, 2025) revoked the Biden-era EO 14110, signaling a shift in federal AI governance approach under the new administration.

**AI-Specific Sentencing Enhancement**: The DOJ Criminal Division has recommended a sentencing enhancement applicable when defendants use AI in commission of an offense.

#### Federal Circuit Court Rulings

No federal circuit court has yet issued a definitive ruling on the admissibility of AI-assisted investigative analysis. The closest analogues involve:
- Challenges to algorithmic risk assessment tools (e.g., COMPAS recidivism scores)
- Challenges to facial recognition identification evidence
- DNA probabilistic genotyping software challenges (discussed above)

### 2.3 State-Level AI Evidence Frameworks

#### Louisiana: Act 250 (First-in-Nation AI Evidence Law)

**Bill**: HB 178
**Act**: Act 250 of the 2025 Regular Legislative Session
**Signed**: June 11, 2025, by Governor Jeff Landry
**Effective**: August 1, 2025

Key provisions:
- Adds new paragraph C to Article 371 of the Louisiana Code of Civil Procedure
- Requires attorneys to use **"reasonable diligence"** to verify the authenticity of evidence before offering it to the court
- Parties in civil cases may raise concerns about admissibility if they suspect evidence was generated or altered by AI
- Attorneys who **know** their exhibits are AI-generated or altered must disclose to opposing counsel
- Concerns about falsified exhibits must be raised "at the pretrial conference or at a pretrial hearing on the admissibility"

Act 250 takes a balanced approach, providing clarity without imposing heavy restrictions on beneficial AI applications in criminal justice. It primarily addresses falsified/fabricated evidence, not legitimate AI-assisted analysis.

#### Colorado: SB24-205 (Consumer Protections for AI)

Colorado's Anti-Discrimination in AI Law (ADAI), signed May 17, 2024, is the first comprehensive U.S. state law governing AI systems affecting consumers.

- **Effective date**: Originally February 1, 2026; **delayed to June 2026**
- **Scope**: High-risk AI systems making consequential decisions in financial services, insurance, healthcare, higher education, housing, government services, **legal services**, and employment
- **Key obligation**: Developers and deployers must use "reasonable care" to avoid algorithmic discrimination
- **Enforcement**: Colorado Attorney General
- **Relevance to ECC**: While ADAI focuses on consumer-facing algorithmic discrimination, its framework for defining "high-risk AI" and requiring bias evaluation establishes a template that may influence evidence law

#### Florida-Specific AI Provisions

Beyond the 11th and 17th Circuit administrative orders on AI disclosure (discussed above), Florida does not yet have comprehensive AI evidence legislation. However, the Florida Supreme Court's ongoing review (SC2025-0659) of Evidence Code amendments may address AI evidence issues.

#### ABA Guidance: Formal Opinion 512

The American Bar Association Standing Committee on Ethics and Professional Responsibility released **Formal Opinion 512** (July 29, 2024), addressing lawyers' use of generative AI:

- **Competence**: Lawyers must independently verify AI outputs used in client representation
- **Confidentiality**: Informed client consent required before entering client information into self-learning AI
- **Fees**: Lawyers may not charge clients for time spent educating themselves on regularly-used AI tools
- **Supervisory duties**: Apply to AI-assisted work product

### 2.4 The Algorithm Disclosure Problem

#### Confrontation Clause Implications

The Sixth Amendment's Confrontation Clause guarantees a criminal defendant's right to confront witnesses. When algorithmic output functions as testimony (identifying suspects, calculating probabilities, drawing inferences), defendants have a constitutional interest in examining the methodology.

**Key precedent on proprietary algorithm disclosure**:

- **TrueAllele/STRmix DNA analysis**: Courts have split on whether defendants are entitled to proprietary source code. Pennsylvania courts denied access, holding TrueAllele is proprietary and could not be marketed if freely available. But other appellate courts have reversed denials, holding that if the state relies on novel probabilistic genotyping software, the defendant is "entitled to access, under an appropriate protective order, to the software's source code and supporting software development and related documentation."

- Even **peer reviewers** of published TrueAllele validation studies have never had access to the source code, undermining the "peer review" prong of Daubert.

- The **Electronic Privacy Information Center** (EPIC) has conducted FOIA campaigns to obtain information about TrueAllele from jurisdictions that use it.

- A **Harvard Journal of Law & Technology** article (Vol. 31, 2018) characterized these tools as "black box algorithms" that threaten defendants' rights.

#### Andrea Roth's "Machine Testimony" Framework

Professor Andrea Roth (UC Berkeley School of Law) published **"Machine Testimony"** in the *Yale Law Journal*, Vol. 126, No. 7 (May 2017), proposing a comprehensive framework for regulating machine evidence:

- Courts currently shoehorn machine evidence into existing rules, treating it as "hearsay," "real evidence," or "methods" underlying human expert opinions — all approaches Roth identifies as "intellectually incoherent."
- Machine sources can be "witnesses" under the Sixth Amendment, and the right of confrontation should focus on **meaningful impeachment** of machine evidence.
- Roth proposes new testimonial safeguards for machines including: (1) front-end design, input, and operation protocols; (2) pretrial disclosure and access rules; (3) authentication and reliability rules; (4) impeachment and courtroom testing mechanisms; (5) jury instructions; and (6) corroboration rules.
- She identifies "black box dangers" — human and machine errors causing a machine to be false by design, inarticulate, or analytically unsound.

**Roth also published** "How Machines Reveal the Gaps in Evidence Law" in *Vanderbilt Law Review*, Vol. 76, No. 6, examining how machine evidence exposes deficiencies in existing evidentiary frameworks.

### 2.6 Brady Implications for AI-Assisted Investigation

#### The Brady Obligation

*Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), holds that the prosecution must disclose to the defense any material evidence favorable to the accused, whether exculpatory or impeaching. Suppression of such evidence violates the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

**Three components of a Brady violation** (as clarified in *Strickler v. Greene*, 527 U.S. 263 (1999)):
1. The evidence is **favorable** to the accused (exculpatory or impeaching);
2. The evidence was **suppressed** by the State, willfully or inadvertently; and
3. **Prejudice** resulted — a "reasonable probability" that disclosure would have produced a different outcome.

#### The Kyles Extension: Affirmative Duty

*Kyles v. Whitley*, 514 U.S. 419 (1995), established that:
- The prosecution's Brady obligation includes **favorable evidence known to others acting on the government's behalf**, including police
- The prosecutor has a **duty to learn** of favorable evidence in possession of law enforcement
- Materiality is assessed based on the **cumulative effect** of all suppressed evidence, not item by item
- "The prosecution, which alone can know what is undisclosed, must be assigned the consequent responsibility to gauge the likely net effect of all such evidence and make disclosure when the point of 'reasonable probability' is reached"

#### Brady and AI-Assisted Investigation: Open Questions

When an AI system is used in investigation, several Brady questions arise:

**1. Must the algorithm itself be disclosed?**

If the AI system identifies patterns implicating the defendant, the defense has a plausible argument that the algorithm itself is Brady material — not because the code is "exculpatory" in isolation, but because understanding how the system reaches its conclusions is necessary to meaningfully challenge the evidence. This argument is strongest when:
- The system's methodology could reveal biases or errors
- The system considered and rejected alternative hypotheses
- The system's confidence scores or error rates are material

No court has definitively ruled on this question. However, the trend toward requiring source code disclosure for probabilistic genotyping software (discussed in section 2.4) suggests courts may increasingly require algorithm disclosure.

**2. Must AI-identified exculpatory patterns be disclosed?**

Under *Kyles*, if an AI system operating on behalf of law enforcement identifies patterns favorable to the defense (alternative suspects, alibi corroboration, evidence gaps undermining the prosecution theory), the prosecution arguably has an obligation to disclose those findings. The AI system's outputs are knowledge possessed by agents of the state.

**3. How does ECC's gap analysis interact with Brady?**

ECC's gap analysis is inherently exculpatory in function. By identifying:
- **Untested evidence** (DNA samples not processed, fingerprints not run)
- **Uninterviewed witnesses** who may provide alibi or alternative narrative
- **Unverified alibis** that remain unchecked
- **Alternative suspect patterns** that the evidence graph surfaces
- **Coherence gaps** in the prosecution's theory

...the system systematically surfaces the very information Brady requires the prosecution to disclose. This is a significant legal and ethical advantage: ECC is architecturally incapable of suppressing exculpatory patterns because gap identification is a core function, not an afterthought.

**4. Documentation obligation**

Under *Strickler v. Greene*, the prosecution cannot hide behind an "open file" policy if exculpatory material is buried in voluminous data. If ECC generates exculpatory findings, those findings must be affirmatively identified and disclosed, not merely included in a data dump. ExoChain's structured audit trail facilitates this: every analytical conclusion is logged with its basis, making it straightforward to identify and disclose favorable findings.

## Topic 3: Building the Admissibility Case for ECC

### 3.1 Testability

**Daubert Factor 1: Has the theory/technique been tested?**

ECC's methodology is inherently testable across multiple dimensions:

**Mathematical Foundation**
- The causal graph methodology rests on well-established mathematical principles: graph theory, spectral analysis, community detection algorithms, and HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) similarity search
- Coherence scoring produces quantitative outputs (numerical scores) that can be validated against known outcomes
- Gap analysis identifies measurable absences in the evidence graph — gaps are either present or not, and their identification can be tested against human expert assessment

**Empirical Testability**
- The system can be tested against cases with known outcomes: run ECC on a resolved case and compare its gap identification, pattern detection, and coherence scores against the actual investigative findings
- False positive and false negative rates for gap identification can be computed by running the system against a corpus of cases where the "ground truth" is known
- Cross-validation is possible: train on one set of cases, test on a held-out set

**Reproducibility**
- Given identical inputs and parameters, ECC produces identical outputs — a fundamental requirement for testability
- The ExoChain audit trail enables exact reproduction of any analytical run
- Open-source code means any qualified researcher can execute the same analysis

**Recommended Validation Protocol**:
1. Obtain 20-50 resolved cold cases with comprehensive documentation
2. Blind input: enter case evidence without informing the system of the resolution
3. Measure: (a) did the system identify the actual evidentiary gaps that led to resolution? (b) did the system surface the key patterns? (c) what was the false positive rate for gap identification? (d) did coherence scores correlate with case strength?
4. Independent replication by external researchers

### 3.3 Error Rate Documentation

**Daubert Factor 3: Known or potential error rate**

Establishing documented error rates is critical for Daubert admissibility. ECC must define, measure, and publish the following metrics:

#### Types of Errors to Measure

| Error Type | Definition | Measurement Method |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Gap detection false positive** | System identifies a gap that does not exist (e.g., flags evidence as "untested" when it was tested) | Compare against ground truth from resolved cases |
| **Gap detection false negative** | System fails to identify a real gap | Compare system output against expert investigator assessment of same case |
| **Pattern matching false positive** | System identifies a cross-case pattern that is coincidental, not meaningful | Statistical significance testing; expert review panel |
| **Pattern matching false negative** | System misses a genuine cross-case connection | Compare against known case linkages (e.g., confirmed serial cases) |
| **Coherence score calibration** | Do higher coherence scores actually correlate with stronger cases? | Correlation analysis: coherence scores vs. prosecution outcomes |
| **Causal link accuracy** | Are inferred causal relationships between evidence nodes justified? | Expert panel review of system-generated causal links |

#### Error Rate Standards from Comparable Forensic Methods

For context, established forensic methods have the following approximate error rates:

- DNA profiling (single-source): less than 1 in 1 billion random match probability
- DNA probabilistic genotyping (mixtures): varies; TrueAllele claims less than 0.01% false inclusion rate (independently unverified due to proprietary code)
- Fingerprint examination: approximately 0.1% false positive rate (FBI-NIJ studies)
- ShotSpotter acoustic detection: company claims 97% accuracy (independently disputed)

ECC should target and document error rates for each function, with the understanding that the system is an investigative support tool (identifying leads and gaps) rather than a definitive forensic identification tool (matching DNA or fingerprints).

#### Standards Maintenance

Daubert also requires "the existence and maintenance of standards controlling the technique's operation." For ECC:
- Version control: all code changes tracked in Git with semantic versioning
- Configuration standards: documented parameter settings for each deployment
- ExoChain verification: tamper-evident logging of every analytical step
- Input data quality standards: documented requirements for evidence ingestion

### 3.6 Open Source as Legal Strategy

#### The Transparency Dividend

Every admissibility challenge to proprietary forensic tools centers on a single problem: the defense cannot examine how the tool reaches its conclusions. ECC eliminates this problem entirely.

**Practical implications**:

1. **No protective order litigation**: Defense counsel can access the full source code from the public repository. This eliminates months of discovery disputes that plague TrueAllele and similar tools.

2. **Defense experts can validate**: Any qualified computer scientist or statistician can review the code, run the same analysis, and verify (or challenge) the results. This is impossible with proprietary tools.

3. **Pre-trial Daubert hearings become simpler**: When the court asks "Can this methodology be tested?" the answer is demonstrably yes — anyone can test it. When the court asks about error rates, the validation studies are reproducible. When the court asks about peer review, the code itself has been subject to the ultimate peer review: open-source community scrutiny.

4. **Confrontation Clause is satisfied**: The defense can confront the methodology directly. There is no "black box" to hide behind and no trade-secret claim to assert.

5. **Prosecutorial advantage**: Paradoxically, open source is a prosecutorial advantage. The prosecution can affirmatively argue: "This tool is transparent. The defense has had full access to the code, the data, and the audit trail. They could have hired their own expert to run the same analysis. The methodology is not hidden."

#### The Garrett-Rudin "Glass Box" Argument

The PNAS article "Interpretable Algorithmic Forensics" (Garrett & Rudin, 2023) provides direct academic support for ECC's approach:

> "Glass box" AI — designed to be interpretable — can be more accurate than black box alternatives, and black box AI performs predictably worse in settings like the criminal system.

This is not merely an argument from principle. It is an empirical finding published in one of the world's leading scientific journals. ECC's interpretable, auditable design is aligned with the emerging scientific consensus on forensic AI.

#### Risk: The Validation Gap

Open-source availability is necessary but not sufficient for admissibility. The primary risk is that courts may view the lack of commercial validation infrastructure (professional training programs, certification bodies, vendor support) as a weakness. The PLOS One study on open-source forensic tools (2025) found that "courts typically favor commercially validated solutions because of the absence of standardized validation frameworks for open-source alternatives."

**Mitigation**: Establish formal validation through academic partnerships, NIJ-funded studies, and law enforcement pilot programs (see sections 3.2-3.4). Create training and certification programs for analysts using ECC. Publish validation results in peer-reviewed journals.
