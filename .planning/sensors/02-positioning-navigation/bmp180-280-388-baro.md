## Overview

Bosch's BMP family are small barometric pressure sensors that double as relative altimeters.
BMP180 is legacy (EOL, 5 Pa resolution). BMP280 is the common I²C part today (0.16 Pa). BMP388
is the current drone-grade part (0.03 Pa = ~25 cm altitude), with BMP390 adding improved noise
and accuracy. All three talk I²C or SPI at 3.3 V.

## Key specs

| Spec | BMP180 | BMP280 | BMP388 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pressure range | 300–1100 hPa | 300–1100 hPa | 300–1250 hPa |
| Resolution | 1 Pa | 0.16 Pa | 0.016 Pa |
| Relative accuracy | ±12 Pa | ±12 Pa | ±8 Pa |
| Rate | 128 Hz | 157 Hz | 200 Hz |
| Bus | I²C | I²C / SPI | I²C / SPI |

## Interface & wiring

Four-wire I²C; BMP280 defaults to 0x76 or 0x77 (SDO pin), BMP388 at 0x77/0x76. On the BMP280+
you can also use SPI for high-rate flight-control loops.

## Benefits

- Tiny, low-power, cheap.
- Enough resolution (BMP388) to detect a single floor change indoors.
- Same driver pattern across the family — easy upgrade path.

## Limitations / gotchas

- *Absolute* altitude is meaningless without a sea-level reference (QNH) — barometric altimeters
  are relative.
- Weather pressure changes of a few hPa over hours → tens of meters of apparent altitude drift.
- Self-heating from a 3D-printed enclosure near hot electronics biases the temp → pressure curve.
- Direct airflow (e.g. fan in a drone) changes static pressure; shield the port.

## Typical use cases

- Drone altitude-hold (BMP388).
- Indoor floor-change detection.
- Weather-station logging (BMP280).
- GPS altitude smoothing.
